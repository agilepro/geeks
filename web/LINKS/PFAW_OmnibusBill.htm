* A Primer on the Omnibus Appropriations Bill

Each year, Congress is charged with passing 13 spending bills by October 1 to fund the federal government and its programs. The House and Senate were unable to agree on 7 of these spending bills this year, so the leadership combined the 7 into a so-called "omnibus" spending bill. The $820 billion omnibus bill was written primarily by Republican House and Senate members working behind closed doors and includes appropriations for:

  • the departments of Agriculture, Commerce, Justice, State, Labor, Health and Human Services, Education, Transportation, Treasury, Veterans Affairs, and Housing and Urban Development
  • the District of Columbia
  • Foreign Operations and the Judiciary
  • NASA and EPA

Knowing that under House and Senate rules the conference report on the omnibus bill cannot be amended on the floor, the Republican negotiators took the opportunity to subvert the democratic process by, among other things,:

  • inserting a District of Columbia school voucher program that failed to pass the Senate; and,
  • removing legislation passed by the House and Senate that would reverse President Bush's attempt to deny overtime pay to 8 million American workers. (Learn more about this issue at http://www.saveovertimepay.org/.)

Since October 1, Congress has passed "continuing resolutions" to keep the government running while it tries to complete its appropriations duties.

VOUCHER TALKING POINTS


For the benefit of you and your friends, here again are the key reasons to oppose the school voucher program in the omnibus bill, which targets the District of Columbia:

1. There is no requirement that voucher schools hire quality teachers who, at a minimum, have a college degree.

2. There is no requirement that voucher schools submit to the same accountability requirements under the No Child Left Behind Act as public schools or even that voucher students be tested in the exact same manner as public school students.

3. The voucher legislation permits discrimination against students based upon disability, gender, limited English proficiency and academic performance and allows religious discrimination against teachers and other staff.

4. The legislation will not provide real choice to the vast majority of school children and their families, many of whom will be unable to obtain the additional funds to pay for tuition and other private-school costs that are not covered by the voucher, such as transportation, uniforms and books.

5. Vouchers are opposed by many Washington, D.C. clergy, elected officials, parents and residents, with the latest poll indicating that residents oppose a voucher program by a 3-to-1 margin.

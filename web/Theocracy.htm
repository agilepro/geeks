My Webster's Seventh Collegiate (1971) gives this definition of theocracy:

 

theocracy : 1. government of a state by immediate divine guidance or

by officials regarded as divinely guided.

 

As I look at the rhetoric, intent and behavior of elected federal

officials it is clear to me that, by this definition, we currently

live under a theocracy.  Someone should ask our highest leaders

which is more important to them, the will of the people or the will

of God.  I think the members of this list might find the answer of

interest.

 

Of course you can go look at all the legal decisions to show that we

are not "officially" a theocracy, no doubt about it, but this is not my

concern: to me the legalities are a facade.  The fact is that I often

ask people who support the present government which document is more

important to them, the Constitution or the Bible, and the answer is the Bible. 

These are folks who owe their highest allegiance to a document written in a

foreign land before our nation was founded.  And they are running the

country.

 

My concern is not the legalities or definition of what constitutes a

theocracy, but with whom has the offensive in the "cultural war"

which has rampaged for so many decades.  I have written about this ad-

infinitum, and yet I continue to observe alerts on secular lists over the latest

offensive from the right, this time from some measure to give more political

power to religious organizations.  So, as usual, the secular side is now

attempting to gear up some rear-guard action to block yet another initiative

from the theocracy to give theocrats even more power than they already have: 

power which as I write is nearly absolute.

 

On the Christian "church-state" list I frequent I asked them the

other day to tell me who in high federal government, cabinet, supreme court,

etc. is not either Christian or Jewish.  No response, other than that some of

the Christian officials are not sufficiently Christian enough to suit the

members of this fundamentalist list.

 

Look, I understand the need for rationality on a skeptics list, but

the people who control our government are not rational.  They are

theocrats who believe that God guides their decisions: they invariably turn to God

when the going gets tough. (Think of Nixon and Kissinger on the carpet

together -- that is our government in a nutshell.)

 

My opinion is that so long as secular people rely upon the niceties

of legal opinion while ignoring who really governs and why, so long as

seculars wage this rear-guard action of counter-response to

theocratic initiatives, the American theocracy will retain its present status

and power.

 

When you are fat if you shop for larger clothes you can convince

yourself that they look good on you and continue to eat.  If no one in the

secular community is willing to identify the blubber of theocracy in this

country for what it is, we will continue to focus upon better-fitting pants

(protective legal decisions) and think we look just fine in the mirror.

 

By this I mean to say that it doesn't matter whether the government

is by any legal definition a theocracy or not:  the government is what we

say it is.  If you want to change what is happening today you go out and

make the claim, that's all.  Perception is everything, especially in

politics.  Secular people, who tend to be of a scientific and

proof-oriented mind, are too shy about using the rhetorical techniques

which are used against them on a daily basis, and have been for

decades.  This is why they have no power in government.

 

We are living under theocratic leaders in a government where an oath

to God is part of national allegiance.  Our Motto is theocratic, our money

is theocratic, our leadership is theocratic.  What more evidence does

one require?

 

If people who care about this issue would start calling the

government a theocracy and call for a change, then change will come.  Not

until.  Tinkering at the edges doesn't work.

 

-Lance
